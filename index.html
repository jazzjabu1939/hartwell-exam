Company Background
The Hartwell Scenario

Hartwell Consumer Products was founded in 1987 in Columbus, Ohio, by Dennis Hartwell, a former kitchen equipment salesman who believed Americans were being underserved by cheap appliances that broke after two years. He was right. Starting with a single commercial-grade blender built for home use, Hartwell grew steadily into a full line of premium kitchen appliances — blenders, drip and espresso coffee makers, and food processors — sold through specialty kitchen retailers, high-end department stores, and eventually its own direct-to-consumer website.

Today the company posts annual revenue of roughly $400 million, is no longer family-run (a professional CEO, Karen Solis, took over in 2019), but still carries the Hartwell name as a mark of craftsmanship in the US "prosumer" market — serious home cooks, culinary school graduates, and small restaurant owners who want near-commercial quality without a commercial price tag.

The problem: the US market is getting crowded. Competitors have caught up on quality, direct-to-consumer brands are undercutting on price, and the board sees limited room for organic growth at home. At the same time, new US tariffs on imported goods have quietly made it more expensive for Hartwell's foreign-made competitors to compete in the American market — which has given Hartwell breathing room domestically, but also planted a question in the boardroom: if tariffs are reshaping global trade flows, shouldn't Hartwell be playing offense internationally, not just defense at home?

The board has approved the company's first international expansion. You've just been appointed Director of International Strategy. Welcome.

Situation Alpha — Choose Your Market
Two Finalists. One Market.

The board didn't hand you a map with a single red pin on it. They handed you a mandate and a budget, and told you to come back with a recommendation. Over the past six months, you and your team have screened 22 countries across four continents. You've looked at market size, income levels, appliance penetration rates, distribution infrastructure, political stability, and ease of doing business. You've interviewed distributors, talked to expats, read State Department reports, and eaten a lot of bad hotel food.

The list is now down to two finalists. You have to choose one.

Both markets are real opportunities. Both carry real risks. Neither is obviously right or obviously wrong. What matters now is whether you understand what you're getting into — and whether Hartwell has what it takes to succeed in the market you choose.
